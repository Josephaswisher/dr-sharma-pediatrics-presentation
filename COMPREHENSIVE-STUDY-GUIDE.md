## Developmental Milestones

### Normal Development by Age

**Birth - 2 Months**:
- **Gross Motor**: Lifts head when prone
- **Fine Motor**: Hands predominantly fisted, tracks objects to midline
- **Language**: Coos, responds to loud noises
- **Social**: Begins to smile (social smile by 6-8 weeks)
- **Red Flags**: No social smile by 2 months, persistent fisting beyond 3 months

**4 Months**:
- **Gross Motor**: Good head control, rolls front to back
- **Fine Motor**: Reaches for objects, hands mostly open
- **Language**: Laughs, orients to voice
- **Social**: Enjoys looking at faces
- **Red Flags**: Poor head control, no reaching for objects

**6 Months**:
- **Gross Motor**: Sits without support, rolls both ways
- **Fine Motor**: Transfers objects hand-to-hand
- **Language**: Babbles
- **Social**: Stranger anxiety begins
- **Red Flags**: Not sitting by 9 months, no babbling

**9 Months**:
- **Gross Motor**: Crawls, pulls to stand
- **Fine Motor**: Pincer grasp (thumb-index finger)
- **Language**: Says "mama/dada" (non-specific)
- **Social**: Waves bye-bye, plays peek-a-boo
- **Red Flags**: Not crawling, no pincer grasp

**12 Months**:
- **Gross Motor**: Walks (may cruise), stands alone
- **Fine Motor**: 2-finger pincer grasp
- **Language**: 1-3 words, follows simple commands
- **Social**: "Mama/dada" (specific)
- **Red Flags**: Not walking by 18 months, no words

**18 Months**:
- **Gross Motor**: Runs, walks up stairs with help
- **Fine Motor**: Stacks 3-4 blocks, uses spoon
- **Language**: 10-20 words, points to body parts
- **Social**: Imitates household tasks
- **Red Flags**: Not walking, fewer than 6 words

**24 Months (2 Years)**:
- **Gross Motor**: Kicks ball, jumps
- **Fine Motor**: Stacks 6+ blocks, draws vertical line
- **Language**: 2-word phrases, 50+ words
- **Social**: Parallel play
- **Red Flags**: No 2-word phrases, not running

**3 Years**:
- **Gross Motor**: Rides tricycle, goes up stairs alternating feet
- **Fine Motor**: Copies circle, uses utensils well
- **Language**: 3-word sentences, speech 75% intelligible
- **Social**: Knows name, age, gender

**4 Years**:
- **Gross Motor**: Hops on one foot, throws overhand
- **Fine Motor**: Copies square, draws person with 3 parts
- **Language**: Tells stories, speech 100% intelligible
- **Social**: Cooperative play

**5 Years**:
- **Gross Motor**: Skips, balances on one foot 10 seconds
- **Fine Motor**: Copies triangle, draws person with 6 parts
- **Language**: Fluent speech
- **Social**: Friends important, rule-following

### Red Flags Requiring Immediate Evaluation

1. **Regression**: Loss of any previously acquired skill at ANY age
2. **No social smile** by 2 months
3. **Poor head control** by 4 months
4. **Not sitting** by 9 months
5. **No babbling** by 12 months
6. **Not walking** by 18 months
7. **No words** by 18 months
8. **No 2-word phrases** by 24 months
9. **Hand dominance** before 18 months (suggests hemiparesis)
10. **Loss of language/social skills** (autism spectrum)

## Neonatology

### Neonatal Jaundice

**Physiologic Jaundice**:
- Appears 24-72 hours of life
- Peaks day 3-5 (term), day 5-7 (preterm)
- Resolves by 2 weeks (term), 3 weeks (preterm)
- Indirect (unconjugated) hyperbilirubinemia
- No treatment if mild

**Pathologic Jaundice** (One or more of):
- Onset <24 hours of life
- Direct (conjugated) bilirubin >2 mg/dL
- Total bilirubin rising >5 mg/dL/day
- Persists >2 weeks (term) or >3 weeks (preterm)

**Causes of Pathologic Jaundice**:

**Hemolytic**:
- ABO incompatibility (mom O, baby A/B)
- Rh incompatibility (mom Rh negative, baby Rh positive)
- G6PD deficiency
- Hereditary spherocytosis

**Non-Hemolytic**:
- Breastfeeding jaundice (inadequate intake → dehydration)
- Breast milk jaundice (unknown factor in breast milk)
- Polycythemia
- Birth trauma (cephalohematoma → breakdown of RBCs)

**Conjugated Hyperbilirubinemia** (Direct >2 mg/dL):
- **Biliary atresia** (TIME-SENSITIVE! Kasai <60 days)
- Choledochal cyst
- Neonatal hepatitis
- TORCH infections (Toxo, Other, Rubella, CMV, HSV)
- Metabolic (galactosemia, tyrosinemia)

**Workup**:
- Total and direct bilirubin
- Blood type (mom and baby)
- Direct Coombs test
- CBC with reticulocyte count
- Peripheral blood smear
- G6PD level (if high clinical suspicion)

**Treatment**:
- **Phototherapy**: Blue light converts unconjugated bilirubin to water-soluble form
  - Indications: Based on nomogram (age in hours vs bilirubin level)
- **Exchange transfusion**: If phototherapy fails or very high levels
  - Removes bilirubin-laden blood, replaces with donor blood
  - Indication: Total bili >25 mg/dL or signs of acute bilirubin encephalopathy

**Kernicterus** (Bilirubin encephalopathy):
- Unconjugated bilirubin crosses blood-brain barrier
- Deposits in basal ganglia
- Acute: Poor feeding, lethargy, hypotonia, high-pitched cry
- Chronic: Choreoathetoid cerebral palsy, hearing loss, upward gaze palsy

### Fever in Infants

**Age <28 Days**:
- **FULL SEPSIS WORKUP + ADMIT**
- Blood culture, CBC with diff
- Urinalysis + urine culture (catheterized specimen)
- Lumbar puncture → CSF culture, cell count, glucose, protein
- Chest X-ray
- **Empiric Antibiotics**:
  - Ampicillin (Listeria coverage) + Gentamicin
  - ± Cefotaxime (NOT ceftriaxone in neonates - displaces bilirubin)
  - Add Acyclovir if HSV risk factors (maternal lesions, seizures, CSF pleocytosis)

**Why Ampicillin?**
- Group B Streptococcus (GBS) - #1 cause early-onset sepsis
- Listeria monocytogenes - 3rd gen cephalosporins DON'T cover!
- E. coli

**Age 28-90 Days**:
- **Risk stratify** (well-appearing? low-risk criteria?)
- **Low-risk criteria** (Rochester, Philadelphia, Boston criteria):
  - Well-appearing
  - Previously healthy, term infant
  - No focal infection on exam
  - WBC 5,000-15,000/μL
  - Urinalysis normal
  - CSF: <8 WBC/hpf
- **Low risk**: Consider observation with close follow-up OR empiric ceftriaxone
- **Any high-risk features**: Full workup + admit

**Age >3 Months**:
- Focused workup based on exam findings
- Source identified (otitis, pharyngitis) → Treat appropriately
- No source but well-appearing → Consider urine culture, may observe
- Ill-appearing → Broader workup

## Cardiology

### Congenital Heart Disease Overview

**Cyanotic** (R→L shunt, deoxygenated blood to systemic circulation):

1. **Tetralogy of Fallot** (most common cyanotic CHD):
   - **Components**: VSD, overriding aorta, pulmonary stenosis, RVH
   - **Presentation**: "Tet spells" (hypercyanotic episodes with crying/feeding)
   - **CXR**: "Boot-shaped heart"
   - **Treatment**: Surgical repair (close VSD, relieve PS)

2. **Transposition of Great Arteries** (TGA):
   - **Anatomy**: Aorta from RV, pulmonary artery from LV (parallel circuits)
   - **Presentation**: Cyanosis in first hours of life, no murmur
   - **CXR**: "Egg on a string"
   - **Echo**: Parallel great vessels
   - **Emergency**: PGE1 (keeps PDA open), balloon atrial septostomy
   - **Treatment**: Arterial switch operation (first week of life)

3. **Truncus Arteriosus**:
   - Single vessel arising from heart (mix of aorta + PA)
   - VSD always present

4. **Tricuspid Atresia**:
   - No communication between RA and RV
   - Single ventricle physiology

5. **TAPVR** (Total Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Return):
   - Pulmonary veins drain to RA instead of LA

**Acyanotic** (L→R shunt, increased pulmonary blood flow):

1. **VSD** (most common CHD overall):
   - **Murmur**: Loud holosystolic at left lower sternal border
   - **Small VSDs**: May close spontaneously
   - **Large VSDs**: CHF, failure to thrive, frequent respiratory infections
   - **Complications**: Eisenmenger syndrome (pulmonary HTN → shunt reversal)

2. **ASD**:
   - **Murmur**: Systolic ejection murmur at left upper sternal border
   - **Exam**: Fixed split S2 (key finding!)
   - **Often asymptomatic** in childhood
   - **Treatment**: Closure (catheter-based or surgical)

3. **PDA** (Patent Ductus Arteriosus):
   - **Murmur**: Continuous "machine-like" murmur
   - **Exam**: Bounding pulses, wide pulse pressure
   - **Premature infants**: May close with indomethacin/ibuprofen
   - **Term infants**: Catheter-based or surgical closure

4. **Coarctation of Aorta**:
   - **Exam**: BP higher in upper extremities, delayed/weak femoral pulses
   - **Murmur**: Systolic murmur at left interscapular area
   - **Associated**: Bicuspid aortic valve, Turner syndrome
   - **Presentation**: CHF in infancy (ductal-dependent) OR HTN in childhood
   - **Treatment**: Surgical repair or balloon angioplasty

5. **Aortic Stenosis**:
   - **Murmur**: Systolic ejection murmur, ejection click
   - **Crescendo-decrescendo quality**
   - **Radiation**: To neck

**Medications in CHD**:
- **PGE1 (Prostaglandin E1)**: Keeps PDA open as "bridge" until surgery
  - Used in: TGA, tricuspid atresia, critical PS, critical AS, coarctation
  - Side effects: Apnea (need intubation/ventilation readiness)

## Respiratory

### Asthma

**Diagnosis** (Clinical):
- Recurrent wheezing, cough, shortness of breath
- Reversible airway obstruction
- Exclude other causes (foreign body, cystic fibrosis, cardiac disease)

**Classification**:
- **Intermittent**: Symptoms ≤2 days/week
- **Mild persistent**: Symptoms >2 days/week but not daily
- **Moderate persistent**: Daily symptoms
- **Severe persistent**: Symptoms throughout the day

**Acute Exacerbation Management**:

**Severity Assessment**:
- **Mild-Moderate**:
  - Can speak in sentences
  - Mild retractions
  - SpO2 >90%
  - Peak flow >50% predicted

- **Severe**:
  - Can only speak in words/phrases
  - Severe retractions, use of accessory muscles
  - SpO2 <90%
  - Peak flow <50% predicted

**Treatment**:

**First-Line**:
- Albuterol 0.15 mg/kg (max 5 mg) nebulized q20min × 3 doses
- Oral corticosteroids: Prednisone 1-2 mg/kg (max 60 mg)

**If not responding**:
- Add ipratropium (anticholinergic) to albuterol
- IV corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 1-2 mg/kg)
- IV magnesium sulfate 50 mg/kg (max 2 g) over 20 min

**Severe/Status Asthmaticus**:
- Continuous albuterol nebulization
- Admit to ICU
- Consider epinephrine, terbutaline, intubation

**Discharge Criteria**:
- Peak flow >70% predicted
- SpO2 >90% on room air
- No accessory muscle use
- Reliably taking PO
- Has inhaler and spacer, knows how to use
- Follow-up arranged

### Bronchiolitis

**Etiology**:
- RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus) - most common
- Peaks in infants 6-12 months during winter months

**Presentation**:
- Prodrome: URI symptoms (rhinorrhea, cough)
- Progression: Wheezing, tachypnea, retractions, poor feeding
- Exam: Diffuse wheezing, crackles, prolonged expiratory phase

**Diagnosis**: Clinical

**Management** (2024 AAP Guidelines):
- **Supportive care ONLY**:
  - Oxygen if SpO2 <90%
  - Nasal suctioning (bulb suction, saline drops)
  - Hydration (PO if tolerating, NG/IV if dehydrated)

**What NOT to do**:
- ❌ Routine albuterol (no proven benefit)
- ❌ Routine corticosteroids (no proven benefit)
- ❌ Routine antibiotics (viral illness)
- ❌ Chest physiotherapy/CPT

**Admission Indications**:
- Age <6 weeks
- Severe respiratory distress
- Apnea
- SpO2 <90% on room air
- Dehydration/poor feeding
- Comorbidities (CHD, chronic lung disease, immunodeficiency)

**Prevention**:
- Palivizumab (Synagis) - RSV monoclonal antibody
- Given monthly during RSV season to high-risk infants:
  - Premature (≤29 weeks gestation)
  - Chronic lung disease
  - Hemodynamically significant CHD

## Gastroenterology

### Bilious Vomiting = Surgical Emergency

**Differential**:
- **Malrotation with volvulus** (MOST IMPORTANT)
- Intestinal atresia/stenosis
- Meconium ileus (cystic fibrosis)

**Malrotation with Volvulus**:
- **Anatomy**: Abnormal intestinal rotation during fetal development
- **Risk**: Midgut twists around SMA → ischemia → necrosis
- **Timing**: Can occur at any age but typically presents in first month
- **Presentation**: Sudden bilious vomiting ± abdominal pain/distension
- **Workup**: Upper GI series (shows abnormal duodenal position, "corkscrew" sign)
- **Management**:
  - NPO, NGT decompression
  - IV fluids (20 mL/kg bolus)
  - STAT surgical consult (Ladd procedure)
  - **Time = bowel viability** (>6 hours → increased necrosis risk)

---

### Pyloric Stenosis

**Epidemiology**:
- Firstborn males
- Age 2-8 weeks (peak 3-5 weeks)

**Pathophysiology**:
- Hypertrophy of pyloric muscle → gastric outlet obstruction

**Presentation**:
- **Vomiting**: Non-bilious, projectile, after feeds
- **Hungry** immediately after vomiting
- **Exam**: Palpable "olive" (RUQ), visible peristaltic waves

**Labs**:
- **Hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis**
- Why? Vomiting HCl → lose H+ and Cl⁻
- Kidneys compensate by reabsorbing HCO₃⁻ (metabolic alkalosis)
- Also: Hypokalemia (vomiting also loses K+)

**Diagnosis**:
- Abdominal ultrasound: Pyloric muscle thickness >3 mm, length >15 mm

**Management**:
- **CRITICAL**: Correct electrolytes BEFORE surgery!
  - IV fluids with KCl
  - Goal: Cl⁻ >100, K+ normal, HCO₃⁻ normalizing
- **Surgery**: Pyloromyotomy (cut through hypertrophied muscle)
- **Prognosis**: Excellent

---

### Intussusception

**Epidemiology**:
- Peak age 6-36 months
- Males > females

**Pathophysiology**:
- Telescoping of intestine (proximal into distal)
- Most common: Ileocolic
- **Lead point** in older kids (>3 years):
  - Meckel diverticulum
  - Lymphoma
  - Polyp
- **Idiopathic** in infants (hypertrophied Peyer patches post-viral)

**Presentation**:
- **Classic triad**:
  1. Colicky abdominal pain (intermittent)
     - Child draws knees to chest, screams
     - Calm/lethargic between episodes
  2. Vomiting (may be bilious if prolonged)
  3. "Currant jelly" stools (blood + mucus)
     - Late finding, indicates ischemia

- **Exam**: RUQ "sausage-shaped" mass

**Diagnosis**:
- Abdominal ultrasound: "Target sign" (or "donut sign")

**Management**:
- **Air or contrast (barium) enema**:
  - Diagnostic AND therapeutic
  - Hydrostatic pressure reduces intussusception in ~80%
- **Contraindications to enema**:
  - Peritoneal signs (perforation)
  - Septic/unstable
- **Surgery**: If enema fails or contraindicated

**Recurrence**: 10-15%

## Infectious Disease

### Bacterial Meningitis

**Etiology** (by age):

**<3 months**:
- Group B Streptococcus (GBS)
- E. coli
- Listeria monocytogenes

**3 months - 18 years**:
- Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common)
- Neisseria meningitidis

**Presentation**:
- **Classic triad**: Fever + headache + neck stiffness
- **Infants**: Nonspecific (irritability, poor feeding, lethargy, bulging fontanelle)
- **Children**: Headache, photophobia, vomiting, altered mental status

**Physical Exam**:
- Kernig sign: Resistance to knee extension when hip flexed
- Brudzinski sign: Neck flexion → hip/knee flexion
- **Meningococcemia**: Petechial/purpuric rash

**Diagnosis**:
- **Lumbar puncture** is GOLD STANDARD
- CSF findings:

| Parameter | Bacterial | Viral | Normal |
|-----------|-----------|-------|--------|
| WBC | >1000 | <500 | <5 |
| Predominant cell | PMN | Lymph | None |
| Glucose | <40 (low) | Normal | >50 |
| Protein | >100 (high) | Slightly ↑ | <45 |
| Gram stain | + in 60-90% | Negative | Negative |

**When to delay LP**:
- Hemodynamic instability
- Signs of increased ICP (papilledema, focal neuro deficits, altered consciousness)
- Overlying skin infection

**⚠️ If LP delayed**: Give antibiotics FIRST (don't delay treatment!)

**Empiric Antibiotics**:

**<3 months**:
- Ampicillin + Gentamicin + Cefotaxime
- Why ampicillin? Listeria coverage

**3 months - 18 years**:
- Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin
- Why vancomycin? Resistant pneumococcus

**Dexamethasone**:
- Give 0.15 mg/kg with or before first antibiotic dose
- Reduces mortality and neurologic sequelae (especially hearing loss)

**Complications**:
- Hearing loss (most common)
- Developmental delay
- Seizures
- Hydrocephalus
- Death

**Prognosis**:
- Mortality without treatment: ~100%
- Mortality with treatment: 10-15%

### Vaccine-Preventable Diseases

**Key Vaccination Milestones**:

**Birth**:
- Hepatitis B (dose #1)
- Give within 24 hours, especially if mother HBsAg+

**2, 4, 6 months**:
- DTaP (Diphtheria, Tetanus, Pertussis)
- IPV (Inactivated Polio Vaccine)
- Hib (Haemophilus influenzae type b)
- PCV13 (Pneumococcal conjugate)
- Rotavirus (oral, live vaccine)

**Rotavirus notes**:
- MUST start by 14 weeks of age
- MUST complete by 8 months
- Do NOT give after 8 months

**12-15 months**:
- MMR #1 (Measles, Mumps, Rubella)
- Varicella #1
- Hepatitis A #1

**4-6 years** (school entry):
- DTaP booster
- IPV booster
- MMR #2
- Varicella #2

**11-12 years**:
- Tdap (Tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis booster)
- MCV4 (Meningococcal conjugate)
- HPV (Human Papillomavirus)
  - 2-dose series if started <15 years
  - 3-dose series if started ≥15 years

**16 years**:
- MCV4 booster (before college)

**Live Vaccines** (contraindicated in immunocompromised):
- MMR
- Varicella
- Rotavirus
- Live intranasal influenza

## Endocrinology

### Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

**Diagnostic Criteria**:
- Glucose >250 mg/dL
- pH <7.3
- HCO₃⁻ <15 mEq/L
- Ketones present (serum or urine)

**Pathophysiology**:
- Insulin deficiency → cells can't use glucose
- Cells break down fat → ketone production
- Ketones → metabolic acidosis

**Presentation**:
- Polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia
- Weight loss
- Abdominal pain, vomiting
- Kussmaul breathing (deep, rapid)
- "Fruity" breath (acetone)
- Altered mental status

**Management** (Pediatric-Specific):

**1. Fluids**:
- **Initial bolus**: 10-20 mL/kg NS (may repeat × 1-2 if shock)
- **Maintenance + deficit**: Replace over **48 hours** (NOT 24!)
- **⚠️ CRITICAL**: Go SLOW to avoid cerebral edema

**2. Insulin**:
- Start 0.1 units/kg/hr IV infusion
- Start AFTER initial fluid bolus
- **DO NOT bolus insulin in children** (adult protocol uses bolus)
- **When glucose <250 mg/dL**:
  - Continue insulin (to clear ketones)
  - Add D5 to IV fluids

**3. Electrolytes**:
- **Potassium**: DKA causes total body K+ depletion (even if serum K+ normal)
- Add K+ to fluids once urine output established and K+ <5.5
- Monitor closely (cardiac arrhythmia risk)

**4. Bicarbonate**:
- Generally NOT recommended
- May worsen hypokalemia and paradoxical CSF acidosis

**Cerebral Edema** (Most feared complication in pediatric DKA):

**Risk Factors**:
- Age <5 years
- New-onset diabetes
- Severe DKA (pH <7.1)
- Rapid fluid administration
- Rapid drop in serum glucose

**Presentation**:
- Headache, altered mental status
- Bradycardia
- Hypertension
- Papilledema

**Timing**: 6-12 hours into treatment

**Treatment**:
- **Mannitol** 0.5-1 g/kg IV over 20 min
- OR Hypertonic saline (3%) 5-10 mL/kg
- Head CT
- ICU transfer

## Neurology

### Status Epilepticus

**Definition**:
- Seizure lasting >5 minutes
- OR Repeated seizures without regaining consciousness between

**Management Protocol** (Time-based):

**0-5 minutes**: SUPPORTIVE CARE
- Position on side (airway protection)
- Ensure patent airway, give oxygen
- IV/IO access
- Check glucose (hypoglycemia is easily treatable cause!)
- If hypoglycemia: D25 2 mL/kg IV (or D10 5 mL/kg)

**5-20 minutes**: BENZODIAZEPINES
- **Lorazepam** 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg)
- If no IV access:
  - Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IM (max 10 mg)
  - OR Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg intranasal
  - OR Diazepam 0.5 mg/kg rectal (max 20 mg)
- May repeat benzodiazepine dose × 1 after 5-10 minutes

**20-40 minutes**: SECOND-LINE AED
- **Fosphenytoin** 20 mg PE/kg IV
- OR **Levetiracetam** (Keppra) 40 mg/kg IV (max 3000 mg)
  - Gaining popularity (fewer side effects than phenytoin)
- OR **Valproate** 40 mg/kg IV

**>40 minutes**: REFRACTORY STATUS EPILEPTICUS
- ICU transfer
- Intubation, mechanical ventilation
- Continuous EEG monitoring
- IV anesthetic agents:
  - Midazolam infusion
  - Pentobarbital
  - Propofol

**Prognosis**:
- Status epilepticus has ~20% mortality if prolonged
- Key is **early aggressive treatment**

---

### Febrile Seizures

**Types**:

**Simple Febrile Seizure**:
- Generalized tonic-clonic
- Duration <15 minutes
- Single seizure in 24 hours
- Age 6 months - 5 years
- No neurologic abnormalities

**Complex Febrile Seizure**:
- Focal features
- Duration >15 minutes
- Multiple seizures in 24 hours
- OR occurs outside age range
- OR post-ictal neurologic abnormalities

**Management**:
- **Simple**: No workup needed, reassure parents
- **Complex**: Consider LP, EEG, imaging

**Recurrence Risk**:
- Overall: 30-40%
- Higher if: <12 months at first seizure, family history

**Risk of Epilepsy**:
- Simple febrile seizure: ~1% (same as general population)
- Complex febrile seizure: 5-10%

**Treatment**:
- Treat underlying fever/infection
- **Antipyretics** (acetaminophen, ibuprofen) for comfort
  - Do NOT prevent recurrent febrile seizures
- **Prophylactic anticonvulsants**: NOT recommended

## Hematology/Oncology

### Sickle Cell Disease

**Pathophysiology**:
- Hemoglobin S (HbS) - abnormal beta-globin chain
- Deoxygenated HbS polymerizes → sickle-shaped RBCs
- Sickled cells: Hemolysis, vaso-occlusion

**Inheritance**: Autosomal recessive
- **HbSS** (homozygous) - most severe
- **HbSC** - milder
- **Sickle cell trait** (HbAS) - generally asymptomatic

**Complications**:

**Vaso-Occlusive Crisis (Pain Crisis)**:
- Most common acute complication
- Triggers: Infection, dehydration, cold, stress
- Pain (bones, abdomen, chest)
- Treatment: Hydration, analgesia (opioids)

**Acute Chest Syndrome**:
- New infiltrate on CXR + fever/respiratory symptoms
- Caused by: Infection, fat embolism, infarction
- Treatment: Antibiotics, oxygen, transfusion, exchange transfusion if severe

**Splenic Sequestration**:
- Sudden pooling of blood in spleen
- Hypovolemia, anemia, splenomegaly
- Can be life-threatening
- Treatment: Transfusion, splenectomy

**Aplastic Crisis**:
- Parvovirus B19 infection → stops RBC production
- Severe anemia
- Treatment: Transfusion

**Stroke**:
- Risk highest in children 2-16 years
- Prevention: Transcranial Doppler screening, chronic transfusions

**Prophylaxis**:
- **Penicillin** prophylaxis (start 2 months, continue until 5 years)
  - Prevents pneumococcal sepsis (functional asplenia)
- **Vaccinations**:
  - Pneumococcal (PCV13 + PPSV23)
  - Hib
  - Meningococcal
  - Annual influenza
- **Hydroxyurea**: Increases HbF, reduces crises

## Child Abuse & Neglect

### TEN-4 Rule (Bruising)

Bruising on:
- **T**orso
- **E**ar
- **N**eck
- In child **<4** months

OR **any bruise** in child <4 months = CONCERNING for abuse

**Why?**
- Pre-mobile infants don't have accidental bruises
- Torso/ear/neck are protected areas (not common sites for accidental trauma)

---

### Fractures Highly Specific for Abuse

1. **Metaphyseal corner fractures** ("bucket-handle" fractures)
   - From twisting/pulling forces
   - Highly specific for abuse

2. **Posterior rib fractures**
   - From squeezing chest
   - NOT from CPR

3. **Multiple fractures at different stages of healing**

4. **Fracture in non-mobile infant**

---

### Shaken Baby Syndrome (Abusive Head Trauma)

**Mechanism**:
- Violent shaking → brain injury

**Classic Triad**:
1. Retinal hemorrhages (bilateral, multi-layered)
2. Subdural hematoma
3. No external signs of trauma

**Presentation**:
- Lethargy, vomiting, seizures, apnea
- May be attributed to "fussiness" or "not acting right"

**Workup**:
- Dilated eye exam (retinal hemorrhages)
- Head CT (subdural hematoma, cerebral edema)
- Skeletal survey (look for other fractures)

---

### Mandatory Reporting

**ALL 50 states**: Healthcare providers are mandated reporters

**You don't need proof** - just **reasonable suspicion**

**Report to**:
- Child Protective Services (CPS)
- Local law enforcement
- Hospital social work

**Document**:
- Objective findings only
- Quote caregiver statements verbatim
- Photograph injuries

## Board-Style High-Yield Facts

1. **Ampicillin for neonatal sepsis**: Covers Listeria (3rd-gen cephalosporins don't!)
2. **Bilious vomiting** = Surgical emergency (malrotation/volvulus)
3. **Jaundice <24 hours** = ALWAYS pathologic
4. **Direct hyperbilirubinemia** = ALWAYS pathologic (biliary atresia!)
5. **Pyloric stenosis**: Fix electrolytes BEFORE surgery
6. **Intussusception**: "Currant jelly" stools = late finding (ischemia)
7. **Bronchiolitis**: Supportive care ONLY (no albuterol/steroids)
8. **Tetralogy of Fallot**: Boot-shaped heart on CXR
9. **TGA**: "Egg on string" CXR, needs PGE1 + balloon septostomy
10. **Fixed split S2** = ASD
11. **Continuous murmur** = PDA
12. **VSD** = Most common CHD overall
13. **Regression** = ALWAYS concerning (evaluate immediately)
14. **Hand dominance <18 months** = Suggests hemiparesis
15. **Cerebral edema** = Leading cause of death in pediatric DKA
16. **DKA**: NO insulin bolus in children, go slow with fluids
17. **Meningitis LP delayed**: Give antibiotics FIRST
18. **Dexamethasone** with meningitis antibiotics (reduces hearing loss)
19. **MMR/Varicella** = Live vaccines (contraindicated if immunocompromised)
20. **Rotavirus**: Must finish by 8 months

1. **Focus on RED FLAGS**: These are life-threatening and board-favorite

4. **Algorithms**: Know these cold (jaundice, fever, bilious vomiting, DKA)
5. **Compare/contrast**: Bacterial vs viral meningitis, simple vs complex febrile seizure
