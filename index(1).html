Growth Charts: Interpretation

Key Concepts

  • Use WHO charts for 0-24 months (based on breastfed infants)
  • Use CDC charts for 2-20 years
  • Plot multiple parameters: Weight, length/height, head circumference (until age 3), BMI (after age 2)
  • Normal variation: Children should track along their percentile curve

Red Flags üö©

Growth Concerns:
  • Crossing 2 major percentile lines
  • Height <3rd percentile or >97th percentile
  • Discordance between parameters (e.g., normal height but low weight)
  • Head circumference <3rd or >97th percentile
Failure to Thrive:
  • Weight <3rd percentile
  • Weight <80% ideal for age
  • Decreased growth velocity
  • BMI <5th percentile (age >2)
üíé Clinical Pearl: Growth pattern more important than absolute percentile. Healthy child at 10th percentile who tracks along their curve is normal. Crossing percentiles = concerning.

Neonatal Jaundice: Evaluation Algorithm

Physiologic vs Pathologic Jaundice

Physiologic (Benign)
  • Appears after 24 hours of life
  • Peaks day 3-5 (term), day 5-7 (preterm)
  • Resolves by 1-2 weeks
  • Total bili <12 mg/dL (term)
  • Unconjugated (indirect)
Pathologic (Concerning)
  • Appears <24 hours of life
  • Rising >5 mg/dL per day
  • Total bili >15 mg/dL
  • Persists >2 weeks (term)
  • Direct bili >2 mg/dL

Evaluation Approach

Jaundice <24 hours old?
‚Üí YES: PATHOLOGIC! Workup: Blood type, Coombs, CBC, retic count, bilirubin
‚Üí Consider: ABO/Rh incompatibility, G6PD deficiency, hereditary spherocytosis
Direct (conjugated) bilirubin >2 mg/dL?
‚Üí YES: Cholestasis workup (biliary atresia, choledochal cyst, infections)
‚Üí Urgent evaluation needed
Jaundice persists >2 weeks (term) or >3 weeks (preterm)?
‚Üí Check direct bilirubin to rule out biliary atresia
‚Üí Consider: Breastfeeding jaundice, hypothyroidism, hemolysis
üíé Clinical Pearl: Phototherapy prevents kernicterus. Exchange transfusion if total bili >25 mg/dL or signs of acute bilirubin encephalopathy. Breastfeeding jaundice (inadequate intake) vs breast milk jaundice (substance in milk) - different entities!
ü¶ã Inova Children's Hospital Slide 13 of 50
Speaker Notes:

Neonatal jaundice is extremely high-yield. Key discriminators: onset <24hr (pathologic), direct hyperbilirubinemia (always pathologic), persistence >2 weeks (check direct bili for biliary atresia). Timing: 6 minutes.

Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)

Pathophysiology

Surfactant deficiency ‚Üí alveolar collapse ‚Üí atelectasis ‚Üí V/Q mismatch ‚Üí hypoxemia

Risk Factors & Clinical Features

Risk Factors:
  • Prematurity (<34 weeks) - #1 risk factor
  • Maternal diabetes
  • C-section without labor
  • Male sex
  • Second-born twin
Clinical Presentation:
  • Tachypnea (>60/min)
  • Grunting (auto-PEEP)
  • Nasal flaring
  • Intercostal/subcostal retractions
  • Cyanosis
Test/Imaging Finding
CXR Ground-glass appearance, air bronchograms, low lung volumes
ABG Hypoxemia, hypercapnia, respiratory acidosis
Lecithin/Sphingomyelin ratio <2:1 indicates lung immaturity

Management

Treatment:
  • Surfactant replacement - give within first hours of life
  • Oxygen therapy, CPAP or mechanical ventilation as needed
  • Prevention: Antenatal corticosteroids (betamethasone) if delivery expected <34 weeks
üíé Clinical Pearl: Maternal steroids (betamethasone or dexamethasone) given 24-48 hours before delivery reduce RDS risk by 50%. Surfactant therapy dramatically improves outcomes - give early!

Tetralogy of Fallot

Most Common Cyanotic Heart Defect

üéØ The 4 Features (PROVe): Pulmonic stenosis | Right ventricular hypertrophy | Overriding aorta | VSD

Clinical Features

  • Boot-shaped heart on CXR (upturned apex from RVH)
  • Tet spells: Hypercyanotic episodes with crying, feeding, defecation
  • Squatting: Increases SVR, decreases R‚ÜíL shunt, improves oxygenation
  • Cyanosis worsens over first year as pulmonic stenosis progresses

Management of Tet Spells

Acute Management (Knee-chest position, Oxygen, Morphine, Fluids, Beta-blockers):
  • Position: Knee-chest (increases SVR)
  • Oxygen
  • Morphine: Decreases respiratory drive, reduces SVR drop
  • IV fluids: Increase preload
  • Beta-blockers: Decrease infundibular spasm
  • Phenylephrine: Increase SVR if refractory
üíé Clinical Pearl: Definitive treatment = surgical repair (typically age 6-12 months). Squatting is characteristic - increases systemic vascular resistance, reducing R‚ÜíL shunt. Tet spells = medical emergency!

Kawasaki Disease

Vasculitis with Coronary Artery Complications

üéØ Diagnostic Criteria: CREAM Conjunctivitis (bilateral, non-exudative) | Rash (polymorphous) | Edema/Erythema of hands & feet | Adenopathy (cervical, >1.5cm) | Mucosal changes (strawberry tongue, cracked lips)
Diagnosis: Fever ‚â•5 days + 4 of 5 criteria above

Key Features

  • Age: Peak 6 months to 5 years, more common in Asian descent
  • Labs: Elevated ESR/CRP, thrombocytosis (platelet >450,000), sterile pyuria
  • Complication: Coronary artery aneurysms (15-25% if untreated)
  • Timeline: Desquamation of fingers/toes occurs in subacute phase (2-3 weeks)

Treatment

IVIG + High-dose Aspirin
  • IVIG: 2 g/kg single dose within 10 days of fever onset
  • Aspirin: High-dose (80-100 mg/kg/day) until afebrile, then low-dose (3-5 mg/kg/day) for antiplatelet effect for 6-8 weeks
  • Goal: Reduce coronary aneurysm risk from 25% to <5%
üíé Clinical Pearl: IVIG must be given within 10 days of fever onset to prevent coronary aneurysms! This is one of the few times aspirin is used in children (Reye syndrome risk elsewhere). Echo to assess coronaries.

Speaker Notes:

Kawasaki is very high-yield. Emphasize the 10-day window for IVIG. Strawberry tongue and desquamation are classic. This is the #1 cause of acquired heart disease in children in developed countries. Timing: 6 minutes.

  • Pathogen: RSV most common (60-80%)
  • Presentation: URI prodrome ‚Üí wheezing, tachypnea, crackles, nasal flaring

20 Asthma: ICS for persistent disease, NOT for acute exacerbations (use SABA + steroids)

Board-Style Practice Questions

Question 1: A 6-week-old infant presents with projectile vomiting after feeds. Labs show pH 7.52, Cl 88, K 3.0, HCO3 32. What is the next best step?

Answer: Abdominal ultrasound. This is classic for pyloric stenosis. Correct electrolytes before surgery.

Question 2: A 4-year-old with 6 days of fever, bilateral conjunctivitis, strawberry tongue, and a rash. What is the most appropriate treatment?

Answer: IVIG + high-dose aspirin. Kawasaki disease - treat within 10 days to prevent coronary aneurysms.

Question 3: A 2-month-old with cough and wheezing. Exam shows nasal flaring and crackles. CXR shows hyperinflation. Most appropriate management?

Answer: Supportive care with nasal suctioning and oxygen. Bronchiolitis - no medications indicated.

Question 4: A 12-month-old is not walking independently and cannot sit without support. What is the concern?

Answer: Significant developmental delay requiring immediate evaluation. Should sit by 9 months latest.

Question 5: A 3-day-old develops jaundice. Total bili 18, direct bili 0.8. Mom is O+, baby is A+. Coombs test is positive. Management?

Answer: Phototherapy. ABO incompatibility with early jaundice. Monitor bilirubin levels.
