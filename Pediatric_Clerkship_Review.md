### **Key Teaching Points (From Dr. Sharma's Experience):**

**Red Flags in Neonates:**
- **Temperature:** Any fever in <28 days = workup (don't assume teething; neonates don't teethe)
- **Feeding:** Refusal to feed often earliest sign of sepsis
- **Lethargy:** Should be sleeping, but arousable; true lethargy = red flag
- **Jaundice:** If progressing rapidly or appears in <24 hours, concerning
- **Respiratory distress:** Tachypnea >60/min, grunting, retractions, nasal flaring
- **Seizures:** In neonate, assume infection until proven (get LP, don't delay)

**Can't-Miss Diagnoses:**
1. **Bacterial Sepsis:** Group B Strep (GBS), E. coli (especially K1), other gram-negatives
2. **Meningitis:** Often presents subtly in neonates (no neck stiffness; just irritability, lethargy)
3. **Congenital Heart Disease:** Cyanosis, murmur, shock state
4. **Inborn Errors of Metabolism:** Hypoglycemia, hyperammonemia, severe metabolic acidosis
5. **Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV):** Vesicles, CNS involvement, disseminated disease

### **Workup Algorithm:**

| Finding | Initial Assessment | Next Step |
|---------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Fever + Well-appearing** | Reassure parents, but labs mandatory | Send home only after negative 48-hour cultures (if low-risk criteria met) |
| **Fever + Ill-appearing** | Admit, empiric antibiotics, LP | Adjust antibiotics based on culture/CSF |
| **Fever + Rash** | Concern for disseminated HSV? Meningococcemia? | LP + empiric IV acyclovir + ceftriaxone |
| **Fever + GI symptoms (vomit, diarrhea)** | Often viral, BUT don't miss bacterial GI (NEC if preemie) | Work up for bacterial causes; monitor for NEC |

### **Dr. Sharma's Clinical Pearl:**
*"I admitted a seemingly well 5-day-old with mom saying 'he's just sleepy.' Turned out to be Group B Strep meningitis. Neonatal sepsis hides behind subtlety. If there's ANY concern, admit and get CSF. Missing meningitis = catastrophic."*

### **Age-Specific Presentations:**

#### **Neonate (0-4 weeks)**

**Pyloric Stenosis:**
- Age: 2-8 weeks (peak 3-5 weeks)
- Presentation: Non-bilious, projectile vomiting after feeding, hungry immediately after
- Exam: **Visible peristaltic wave**, palpable "olive" in epigastrium (right upper quadrant)
- Labs: **Hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis** (vomiting HCl + loss of chloride)
- **CRITICAL BEFORE SURGERY:** Correct electrolytes! If K+ low, post-op apnea risk
- Diagnosis: **Abdominal ultrasound** (pyloric muscle >3mm, channel >14mm)
- Treatment: **Pyloromyotomy** (open or laparoscopic)

**\*\*Dr. Sharma's Teaching Moment:\*\*** "I see students order IV fluids, then rush to OR. **Stop.** Recheck electrolytes after hydration. This baby needs chloride repletion‚Äîgive 3% saline if K+ very low. Rushing to OR on a kid with profound hypokalemia = post-op complications."

**Duodenal Atresia:**
- Age: First days of life
- Presentation: Bilious vomiting (always), often polyhydramnios in pregnancy history
- Associated: Down syndrome (~50% of cases)
- Imaging: **"Double bubble" sign** on abdominal X-ray (air in stomach + proximal duodenum)
- Treatment: **Duodenoduodenostomy** (surgical bypass)

**Esophageal Atresia/Tracheoesophageal Fistula (EA/TEF):**
- Age: Immediately at birth (or within hours of first feed)
- Presentation: Excessive drooling, respiratory distress with feeds, inability to pass NGT
- Associated: **VACTERL associations** (Vertebral, Anorectal, Cardiac, Tracheoesophageal, Renal, Limb)
- Imaging: **CXR with radiopaque dye** (look for coiling of tube in esophagus)
- Treatment: **Surgical repair** (often multi-stage depending on anatomy)

#### **Infant (1-12 months)**

**Most Common: Gastroenteritis (Viral)**
- Presentation: Acute onset, often with diarrhea, fever, URI prodrome
- Management: **Supportive** (ORS, continue feeding, monitor for dehydration)
- Red flags: Severe dehydration (>10% weight loss), bloody diarrhea, altered mental status

**Intussusception:**
- Age: Peak 6-36 months
- Presentation: **Colicky abdominal pain + vomiting + diarrhea ("currant jelly" stools)**
- Palpable mass: **Right upper quadrant "sausage-shaped" mass** on exam (30% palpable)
- Imaging: **Abdominal ultrasound** (gold standard; shows "target sign")
- Treatment: **Reduction** (air or hydrostatic enema‚Äînon-operative if no perforation signs)
- Risk of perforation: Monitor closely; if signs of peritonitis ‚Üí surgical reduction

**\*\*Key Clinical Pearl:\*\*** "Intussusception is classic test answer, but I see it twice a year. Gastroenteritis is every week. BUT‚Äîwhen you see a 10-month-old with colicky pain and currant jelly stools, think intussusception and get ultrasound. Don't delay."

#### **Toddler (1-3 years)**

**Gastroenteritis (Most Common)**
- Presentation: Viral (rotavirus, norovirus, adenovirus) or bacterial (Salmonella, Shigella)
- Management: Supportive care, continue feeding (PO rehydration if mild-moderate)
- Antibiotics: **NOT routine** (unless specific bacterial pathogen with systemic signs)
- Red flag: Bloody diarrhea with fever + toxic appearance ‚Üí Consider antibiotics

**Appendicitis:**
- Presentation: Periumbilical pain ‚Üí right lower quadrant, vomiting, fever, constipation common
- Classic exam: **McBurney's point tenderness**, Rovsing's sign, rebound tenderness
- Imaging: **Ultrasound or CT** (CT more sensitive but radiation; US if equipment available)
- Treatment: **Appendectomy** (open or laparoscopic)

**Obstructive Causes (Malrotation, Adhesions):**
- Presentation: Bilious vomiting (always escalate!), abdominal distension, failure to thrive if chronic
- History: Prior abdominal surgery? Prematurity?
- Imaging: **Upper GI series** (shows abnormal duodenal placement in malrotation)
- Treatment: **Surgical correction** (Ladd procedure for malrotation)

#### **School-Age Child (>5 years)**

**Appendicitis (Most Common Surgical Abdomen)**
- Higher suspicion here; kids can communicate pain better
- Presentation: RLQ pain, anorexia, vomiting, fever
- Imaging: **CT abdomen/pelvis** (gold standard; ultrasound reasonable first-line)
- Treatment: **Appendectomy**

**Peptic Ulcer Disease / Gastritis:**
- Presentation: Vomiting (may be coffee-ground if hemorrhagic), epigastric pain
- Etiology: H. pylori (most common worldwide; less common in developed countries), NSAIDs
- Workup: H. pylori testing (serology, urea breath test, or endoscopy if high suspicion)
- Management: PPI, H. pylori eradication if positive

**Functional Vomiting / Cyclic Vomiting Syndrome (CVS):**
- Presentation: Recurrent episodes of vomiting separated by symptom-free intervals
- Triggers: Stress, specific foods, infections
- Red flags to rule out: Weight loss, blood in vomit, morning vomiting only (suggests increased ICP)
- Management: Supportive, preventive medications (tricyclic antidepressants, propranolol)

### **Universal Vomiting Workup (When to Be Thorough):**

```
FOR ANY CHILD WITH VOMITING:
‚òê History: Onset, bilious?, blood?, associated symptoms, feeding pattern, prior surgery
‚òê Exam: Dehydration assessment, abdominal distension/tenderness, peristalsis, masses, hernia
‚òê Labs if indicated: Electrolytes (hyponatremia from SIADH?), glucose, liver function
‚òê Imaging: Usually start with abdominal X-ray; escalate based on presentation
‚òê Red Flags Demanding Urgent Imaging: Bilious vomiting, severe pain, distension, prior surgery
‚òê Escalation: If surgical abdomen suspected ‚Üí Surgical consultation
```

### **Dr. Sharma's Hospitalist Wisdom:**

*"As a resident, I over-investigated vomiting. Now I know: **Bilious = surgical.** **Non-bilious = try supportive care first unless red flags.** This filters out unnecessary CTs and speeds diagnosis. Also, always ask about prior surgeries‚Äîadhesions are sneaky."*

### **Opening Challenge:**
> "3-month-old, temp 39¬∞C, otherwise well. 2-year-old, temp 38¬∞C, nonspecific complaints, no source found. 5-year-old, temp 37.8¬∞C, cough. **Same vital sign, three different workup levels.** Why?"

### **The Age-Based Fever Algorithm**

**Key Principle:** **Fever risk stratification is age-dependent. Younger = more investigation.**

#### **Fever in Neonates (<28 days)**

```
ANY FEVER ‚â•38¬∞C RECTAL
    ‚Üì
    MANDATORY FULL EVALUATION (High risk of serious bacterial infection)
    ‚îú‚îÄ Blood culture
    ‚îú‚îÄ CBC with differential
    ‚îú‚îÄ Urinalysis + urine culture (clean-catch or cath)
    ‚îú‚îÄ Lumbar puncture (CSF culture, protein, glucose, Gram stain)
    ‚îú‚îÄ CXR (rule out pneumonia, other respiratory pathology)
    ‚îî‚îÄ Empiric antibiotics: Ampicillin + gentamicin ¬± ceftaxime
    
    Admit to hospital (minimum observation)
```

**\*\*Why ampicillin for neonates?\*\*** Listeria monocytogenes is covered by ampicillin but NOT by 3rd-gen cephalosporins. This is unique to neonates and young infants‚Äîa classic exam question AND a real management issue.

#### **Fever in Infants 28-90 days (High-Risk Period)**

**Low-Risk Criteria (can consider outpatient management with close follow-up; rare):**
- Well-appearing, no focal source
- WBC 5-15 √ó 10¬≥, **normal U/A**, CSF normal if done
- **BUT:** Most clinicians still admit for observation (safer)

**High-Risk / Unwell:**
- Admit, full fever workup, empiric antibiotics
- Concern for UTI, meningitis, bacteremia

#### **Fever in Infants 3-36 months (Variable Risk)**

**Well-Appearing, No Source Found:**
- **Occult bacteremia** risk: 3-5% in this age group (decreased with Hib/PCV vaccines)
- Workup consideration:
  - Consider **urine culture** (UTI common, can be only source)
  - **Blood culture** if high fever (‚â•39¬∞C) + elevated WBC
  - **CXR** if respiratory signs
- Management:
  - If source found (otitis, pharyngitis): Appropriate antibiotics
  - If no source, well-appearing: **Can observe closely with follow-up** OR give empiric antibiotics (institution-dependent)
  - Admit if: ill-appearing, immunocompromised, unreliable follow-up

**\*\*Dr. Sharma's Clinical Decision:\*\*** "At Inova, we're conservative. Well-appearing 18-month-old with fever, no source, normal labs? I give empiric amoxicillin-clavulanate and see them in clinic in 24 hours. Reduces unnecessary admissions while keeping kids safe."

#### **Fever in Children >3 years (Lower Risk)**

**Well-Appearing:**
- Occult bacteremia rare
- Often viral URI
- Workup: Focused history/exam; lab only if indicated by findings
- Management: Supportive care, fever management, close follow-up

**Ill-Appearing or Focal Findings:**
- Workup based on presentation (e.g., strep throat ‚Üí rapid strep + culture; pneumonia ‚Üí CXR)
- Antibiotics targeted to suspected pathogen

### **Red Flags in Any Febrile Child:**

```
üö® ALWAYS ESCALATE if:
‚òê Petechial or purpuric rash (meningococcemia until proven)
‚òê Altered mental status, lethargy, or poor response to stimulation
‚òê Signs of shock (tachycardia, hypotension, delayed cap refill, decreased urine output)
‚òê Difficulty breathing (stridor, severe cough)
‚òê Seizures (febrile or afebrile)
‚òê Severe headache + neck stiffness (meningitis)
‚òê Age <28 days with any fever
‚òê Immunocompromised child (cancer, HIV, transplant, asplenia)
```

### **Febrile Seizure (Common in 6 mo - 5 years):**

**Definition:** Seizure provoked by fever (usually 38-40¬∞C), occurs in ~3-5% of children

**NOT febrile seizure if:**
- No fever present
- Afebrile seizure history
- Neurologic abnormality
- Prolonged seizure (>15 min)

**Workup:**
- First febrile seizure: **LP recommended** (to rule out meningitis) if age <18 months OR **signs of meningitis**
- If LP done and CSF normal: Reassurance
- Imaging (CT, MRI): Usually NOT needed unless abnormal neuro exam

**Management:**
- **Antipyretics** don't prevent recurrent febrile seizures (but make kid comfortable)
- **Prophylactic antiepileptics:** Usually NOT used (benzodiazepines reserved for high-risk cases)
- **Parental counseling:** Very good prognosis, no long-term developmental consequences
- **Follow-up:** Neurologist if recurrent (>3 episodes) or atypical features

### **Critical Red Flags by System:**

| System | Red Flags | Action |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Respiratory** | Stridor, severe retractions, SpO‚ÇÇ <90%, apnea, cyanosis | Airway assessment, oxygen, consider ICU |

### **Escalation Pathways (Know Your Hospital System):**

**At Inova Hospital:**
- **RRT (Rapid Response Team):** Called for respiratory distress, altered mental status, shock
- **Pediatric Surgery Consult:** Any bilious vomiting, acute abdomen, trauma
- **PICU Admission:** Respiratory support needs, hemodynamic instability, altered mental status, post-surgical complications

---

### **Common Respiratory Presentations:**

#### **Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)**

**Etiology:** Viral, usually parainfluenza virus

**Presentation:**
- Prodrome: Upper respiratory symptoms (nasal congestion, mild fever)
- Classic: **Barky, seal-like cough** + **stridor** (especially at night)
- Age: 6 months - 3 years (peak)

**Severity Grading:**
- **Mild:** Occasional cough, no stridor at rest
- **Moderate:** Frequent cough, stridor at rest, no respiratory distress
- **Severe:** Stridor at rest + respiratory distress, hypoxia

**Management:**
- **All children:** Dexamethasone (0.6 mg/kg √ó 1 dose, max 10mg) ‚Äî reduces symptoms, duration, recurrence
- **Mild-Moderate:** Outpatient if reliable follow-up; discharge with caregiver counseling
- **Severe:** Admit, consider epinephrine (racemic or L-epinephrine), oxygen
- **Home care:** Humidified air, reassurance (very low mortality with treatment)

**\*\*Dr. Sharma's Pearl:\*\*** "Parents are scared of croup (that cough sounds awful!). I tell them: 'This is viral, we give one steroid dose, and it gets better in 3-5 days. Very treatable.' Dexamethasone is magic here."

#### **Bronchiolitis (RSV, other respiratory viruses)**

**Etiology:** Viral (RSV most common), causes inflammation of small airways

**Presentation:**
- Age: <2 years (peak 6-12 months)
- Prodrome: URI symptoms
- Progressive: Cough, tachypnea, wheezing or crackles on auscultation
- Severe: Respiratory distress, hypoxia, apnea (especially preemies, <6 weeks)

**2024-2025 Guideline Update:** **Albuterol and steroids NOT routinely recommended** (evidence doesn't support routine use)

**Management:**
- **Supportive:** Oxygen if SpO‚ÇÇ <90%, nasal suctioning, hydration
- **Hospitalization indication:** Respiratory distress, SpO‚ÇÇ <90%, apnea, age <6 weeks, severe comorbidities (prematurity, congenital heart disease, immunodeficiency)
- **Avoid:** Routine antibiotics (viral!), routine steroids/albuterol
- **Ribavirin:** Reserved for severely immunocompromised (rare)

**Prognosis:** Most recover in 5-7 days; some cough lingers weeks

**\*\*Key Teaching:\*\*** "I see students reflexively giving albuterol to bronchiolitis. Stop. We don't do that anymore. Supportive care‚Äîoxygen, monitoring, hydration‚Äîis the mainstay. Antibiotics only if bacterial superinfection suspected (fever recurrence, focal findings)."

#### **Pneumonia**

**Etiology (Age-Dependent):**
- **<5 years:** Viral (RSV, influenza, parainfluenza) most common; bacterial (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae) less common
- **School-age:** Bacterial (S. pneumoniae, M. pneumoniae, atypicals) more common

**Presentation:**
- Fever, cough (often productive in older kids), tachypnea, chest pain (if pleurisy), dyspnea
- Exam: Crackles/rales on auscultation (especially lower lobes), possible consolidation signs

**Diagnosis:**
- **CXR:** Shows infiltrate (lobar, multilobar, or bronchopneumonia pattern)
- Labs: CBC (elevated WBC if bacterial), blood culture (if admitted)
- **Viral testing:** Rapid tests for RSV, influenza, COVID-19

**Management (Viral vs. Bacterial):**

| Presentation | Likely Etiology | Antibiotic? |
|--------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Fever, URI symptoms, viral study positive | Viral pneumonia | NO (supportive only) |
| High fever, lobar infiltrate, elevated WBC, toxic appearance | Likely bacterial | YES (amoxicillin PO or ceftriaxone IV) |
| Cough 2+ weeks, negative exam but constitutional symptoms | M. pneumoniae (atypical) | YES (azithromycin) |

**Admission Criteria:**
- Respiratory distress
- SpO‚ÇÇ <90%
- Age <6 months
- Severe comorbidities
- Inability to take oral antibiotics
- Immunocompromised

### **Viral Gastroenteritis (The Most Common)**

**Etiology:** Rotavirus (less common post-vaccine), norovirus, adenovirus, astrovirus

**Presentation:**
- Acute onset diarrhea, vomiting, fever
- Usually 1-7 days duration
- Abdominal cramping common

```

‚îú‚îÄ **Lactose-free formula** if on formula (temporary)
‚îú‚îÄ **Avoid:** High-sugar drinks (apple juice, sports drinks)
‚îú‚îÄ **Probiotics:** Modest evidence for Lactobacillus GG (may shorten duration 1-2 days)
‚îú‚îÄ **Zinc supplementation:** In developing countries (limited data in developed countries)
‚îî‚îÄ **Antibiotics:** NO (viral; no benefit; may harm gut flora)

ADMISSION CRITERIA:
‚òê Severe dehydration (‚â•10% weight loss, shock)
‚òê Unable to take PO (severe vomiting despite antiemetics)
‚òê Bloody diarrhea (rare but concerning)
‚òê Age <6 months (higher risk of complications)
‚òê Immunocompromised
‚òê Unreliable follow-up
```

**Dehydration Assessment:**

| Mild (3-5%) | Moderate (6-9%) | Severe (‚â•10%) |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| Normal thirst, normal cap refill | Increased thirst, normal cap refill | Extreme thirst, delayed cap refill |
| Normal urine output | ‚Üì urine output | ‚Üì‚Üì urine output |

### **Failure to Thrive (FTT) / Malnutrition**

**Definition:** Weight <3rd percentile for age OR crossing growth percentile lines downward

**Etiology (GI-Focused):**
- **Medical causes:** Celiac disease, IBD, CF, food allergy, reflux, chronic diarrhea
- **Nutritional causes:** Inadequate caloric intake, poor formula preparation
- **Feeding issues:** Oral motor dysfunction, behavioral (picky eater)
- **Multifactorial:** Usually combination of medical + environmental

**Red Flags for Organic GI Disease:**
- FTT onset <6 months (earlier = more likely organic)
- Vomiting with feeds, diarrhea (bloody? oily?)
- Abdominal distension or pain
- Family history of celiac, IBD, CF

**Initial Hospitalist Workup:**

```
STEP 1: Assess Severity
‚îú‚îÄ Calculate % weight loss from baseline
‚îú‚îÄ Assess muscle mass, subcutaneous fat (malnutrition severity)
‚îî‚îÄ Signs of deficiency: Hair changes? Skin findings? Edema (kwashiorkor)?

STEP 2: Directed History & Exam
‚îú‚îÄ Feeding history (formula prep? Exclusive breastfed?)
‚îú‚îÄ Stool pattern (consistency, frequency, blood, fat)
‚îú‚îÄ Vomiting pattern, timing relative to feeds
‚îú‚îÄ Exam: Abdominal masses? Organomegaly? Perianal disease?
‚îî‚îÄ Developmental assessment (global delay vs. isolated growth issue?)

STEP 3: Labs (If Organic Etiology Suspected)
‚îú‚îÄ CBC (anemia? MCV‚Äîiron deficiency vs. macrocytic from B12/folate)
‚îú‚îÄ CMP, albumin (protein malnutrition)
‚îú‚îÄ Tissue transglutaminase (celiac screening if indicated)
‚îú‚îÄ Fecal fat (72-hour collection if steatorrhea suspected‚Äîmalabsorption)
‚îî‚îÄ Consider: CF sweat test, cow's milk protein allergy trial

STEP 4: Nutritional Repletion
‚îú‚îÄ Caloric needs: Calculated based on weight + age
‚îú‚îÄ Increase caloric density (add fat, not just calories)
‚îú‚îÄ Consider high-calorie supplements (Pediasure, etc.) if inadequate PO
‚îú‚îÄ If severe malnutrition + inadequate PO ‚Üí Consider feeding tube
‚îî‚îÄ Monitor: Weekly weights, growth trajectory
```

**Admission Indications for FTT:**
- Severe malnutrition requiring supervised refeeding
- Medical complication (dehydration, electrolyte abnormality)
- Suspected abuse/neglect
- Inability to feed at home
- Need for diagnostic procedures (endoscopy for celiac, swallow study)

### **Celiac Disease (New Diagnosis)**

**2024-2025 Update:** Diagnosis can be made **without biopsy** if serology strongly positive.

**Presentation (Variable):**
- GI: Chronic diarrhea ("veggie stools"), abdominal distension, constipation paradoxically
- Systemic: Failure to thrive, fatigue, irritability
- Subtle: May present with just iron deficiency anemia or normal growth with vague symptoms

**Diagnostic Workup:**
1. **Serology first (on regular diet with gluten):**
   - Tissue transglutaminase IgA (tTG-IgA) [MOST SENSITIVE]
   - Total IgA (rule out IgA deficiency)
   - Endomysial antibody (EMA-IgA) for confirmation

2. **Interpretation:**
   - **tTG-IgA ‚â•10x upper limit of normal + EMA positive ‚Üí Diagnosis made (no biopsy needed)**
   - **Intermediate values OR IgA deficiency ‚Üí Need biopsy**

3. **Biopsy (if needed):**
   - Endoscopy with distal duodenal biopsy
   - Shows villous atrophy (Marsh grade 3)
   - Multiple sites important (patchy involvement)

**Management:**
- **Gluten-free diet (STRICT adherence)** ‚Äî ONLY effective treatment
- Screen for micronutrient deficiency: Iron, B12, folate, fat-soluble vitamins, calcium
- Repeat serology 1-2 years (should normalize if compliant)
- Monitor for complications: Osteoporosis risk, dermatitis herpetiformis, other autoimmune conditions
- Dietitian referral ESSENTIAL

**Hospitalist Role:**
- Make diagnosis
- Referral to dietitian for education
- Support in transition to gluten-free lifestyle
- Address nutritional deficiencies before discharge

## **Session 2.3: Cardiac & Neurologic Red Flags (90 minutes)**

### **Cardiac Emergencies You Must Recognize:**

#### **Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) in Children**

**Common Etiology:**
- **Structural:** Ventricular septal defect (VSD), atrial septal defect (ASD), patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)
- **Cardiomyopathy:** Myocarditis (viral), dilated cardiomyopathy (genetic)
- **Arrhythmia:** SVT, complete heart block

**Presentation:**
- **Infants:** Poor feeding, failure to thrive, respiratory distress, hepatomegaly
- **Older kids:** Dyspnea on exertion, orthopnea, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly, ascites

**Exam Findings:**
- Tachycardia, tachypnea
- Cardiomegaly on CXR (heart size >50% of chest diameter)
- Rales on lung exam (pulmonary edema)
- Hepatomegaly (right heart strain)
- Gallop rhythm (S3)

**Workup:**
- **CXR:** Cardiomegaly, pulmonary edema (Kerley B lines)
- **ECG:** May show chamber enlargement, ischemia
- **Echocardiography:** Definitive; shows chamber function, ejection fraction, structural disease

**Management:**
- **Diuretics:** Furosemide for volume overload
- **ACE inhibitors:** Reduce afterload
- **Beta-blockers:** Improve function
- **Treatment of underlying cause:** Surgical correction of structural lesions, antivirals for myocarditis
- Specialist (Cardiology) consultation ESSENTIAL

#### **Arrhythmias**

**Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT) - Most Common Pediatric Arrhythmia**

**Presentation:**
- Infants: Irritability, poor feeding, shock if prolonged
- Older kids: Palpitations, dizziness, chest pain, syncope

**ECG Finding:** Regular, narrow-complex tachycardia, no P waves visible (buried in T wave)

**Acute Management:**
1. **Vagal maneuvers** (older child): Valsalva, ice water immersion (infants)
2. **Adenosine IV:** 0.1 mg/kg rapid push (works by blocking AV node)
   - If unsuccessful: 0.2 mg/kg push
   - Goal: Brief AV node block ‚Üí SVT terminates, sinus rhythm returns
3. **Synchronized cardioversion:** If hemodynamically unstable or adenosine fails

**Follow-up:**
- **Holter monitor / event monitor:** Detect recurrences
- **Cardiology referral:** Consider ablation if recurrent

### **Neurologic Red Flags:**

#### **Seizures**

**Differential Diagnosis:**
- **Febrile seizure** (most common, 6mo-5yr with fever)
- **Generalized tonic-clonic seizure** (epilepsy, metabolic, infection)
- **Absence seizure** (brief staring, no memory; often multiple per day)
- **Complex partial seizure** (behavioral arrest, lip smacking, post-ictal confusion)
- **Status epilepticus** (seizure >5 minutes or repeated seizures without return to baseline) ‚Äî EMERGENCY

**Initial Assessment:**
```
SEIZING CHILD
‚îú‚îÄ Position: Turn on side (prevent aspiration)
‚îú‚îÄ Airway: Ensure patent, give oxygen
‚îú‚îÄ IV access: Draw labs (glucose, electrolytes, CBC, culture if fever)
‚îú‚îÄ Check glucose stat: Hypoglycemia easy to treat
‚îú‚îÄ Fever? ‚Üí Full septic workup (bacterial meningitis risk)
‚îú‚îÄ Rash? ‚Üí Meningococcal concern, empiric antibiotics
‚îî‚îÄ Imaging (CT/MRI): If abnormal neuro exam or status epilepticus
```

**Anticonvulsant Choices:**
- **First-line:** Levetiracetam (Keppra), lacosamide
- **Status epilepticus:** Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV, then fosphenytoin (Cerebyx) or levetiracetam

**Admission Indications:**
- First unprovoked seizure
- Status epilepticus
- Abnormal neuro exam
- Suspected infection (meningitis, encephalitis)

#### **Meningitis (Bacterial)**

**Presentation:**
- Infants: Fever, irritability, lethargy, poor feeding (classic triad of fever + meningismus + altered mental status ABSENT in infants)
- Older kids: Fever, headache, neck stiffness, altered mental status, possible rash (petechiae/purpura)

**Red Flag:** Petechial rash with fever = meningococcemia until proven otherwise

**Diagnosis:**
- **Lumbar puncture (LP):** Gold standard
  - CSF findings: Elevated protein, low glucose, pleocytosis (PMNs)
  - Culture: Grows organism
  - Gram stain: May show organism

**\*\*CRITICAL:\*\*** If meningitis suspected + LP delayed ‚Üí Give antibiotics immediately (don't wait for LP)

**Empiric Antibiotics (Based on Age):**
- **<3 months:** Ampicillin + gentamicin + ceftazidime (covers Listeria)
- **3 months - 18 years:** Ceftriaxone + vancomycin + (add ampicillin if <50 years OLD or immunocompromised for Listeria)
- **Dexamethasone:** 0.15 mg/kg IV (reduces mortality if given with or before first antibiotic dose)

**Prognosis:** Mortality ~10-15%; long-term disability (hearing loss, developmental delay) in survivors

## **Session 3.1: Chronic Conditions Requiring Hospitalization (120 minutes)**

### **Pediatric Asthma Exacerbation ‚Äî Hospitalization & Management**

**Admission Criteria (Moderate-Severe Exacerbation):**
- Peak flow 40-69% predicted
- Moderate-severe dyspnea, speak in phrases
- Respiratory rate elevated for age
- SpO‚ÇÇ 90-94% on room air
- Failure to respond to ER treatment

**Inpatient Management Protocol:**

```
HOSPITAL DAY 1-2: ACUTE PHASE
‚îú‚îÄ Continuous pulse oximetry
‚îú‚îÄ Albuterol: Nebulized continuously (not just q 4h)
‚îú‚îÄ Ipratropium (anticholinergic): Enhances beta-2 agonist effect
‚îú‚îÄ IV/oral steroids: Prednisone 1-2 mg/kg daily (usually 3-5 days total)
‚îú‚îÄ IV magnesium: 50 mg/kg (max 2g) if not responding
‚îú‚îÄ Oxygen: Target SpO‚ÇÇ >90%
‚îî‚îÄ CXR if concerned for pneumothorax or pneumonia

REASSESSMENT (q 4-6 hours):
‚îú‚îÄ Peak flow (trend important)
‚îú‚îÄ Subjective: "Can you talk in full sentences?"
‚îú‚îÄ Objective: RR, retractions, accessory muscle use
‚îî‚îÄ Disposition: If improving ‚Üí Step down to intermittent neb

DISCHARGE CRITERIA (Usually Day 2-3):
‚òê Peak flow >70% predicted
‚òê SpO‚ÇÇ >90% on room air
‚òê No accessory muscle use at rest
‚òê Tolerating PO without distress
‚òê Reliable discharge plan in place
```

**Discharge Instructions:**
- Inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) daily (the key for preventing recurrence)
- Beta-2 agonist PRN for symptoms
- Spacer technique review (many kids use incorrectly)
- Asthma action plan (green/yellow/red zones)
- Trigger identification
- Follow-up with PCP/Pulmonology in 1-2 weeks

### **Dehydration from Chronic Diarrhea ‚Äî Multifactorial Management**

**Common Etiologies Requiring Hospitalization:**
- Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis) ‚Äî flare
- Celiac disease ‚Äî new diagnosis with severe symptoms
- Short bowel syndrome (post-surgical)
- Malabsorption (cystic fibrosis, tropical sprue)

**Hospitalization Goals:**
1. **Rehydrate** with IV fluids (replace deficit + ongoing losses)
2. **Identify/treat underlying cause** (endoscopy for IBD? Biopsy for celiac?)
3. **Nutritional repletion** (high-calorie diet once tolerating PO)
4. **Medication initiation** (if IBD ‚Üí start appropriate therapy)

**IV Rehydration Protocol:**
- **Bolus:** 20 mL/kg normal saline over 1 hour (reassess perfusion)
- **Maintenance + ongoing losses:** Calculated based on urine output, stool losses
- **Electrolyte repletion:** Based on labs (hyponatremia, hypokalemia common)

**Dietary Management (Once Tolerating PO):**
- Clear liquids ‚Üí Regular diet (advance as tolerated)
- High-calorie (add fats), lactose-free if appropriate
- Small, frequent meals
- Probiotics: Controversial; some evidence for Lactobacillus GG
